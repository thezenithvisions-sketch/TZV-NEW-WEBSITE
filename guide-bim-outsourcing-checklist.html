The BIM Outsourcing Checklist

A practical, vendor-agnostic checklist for scoping, choosing and running a BIM outsourcing partner, so your first project lands clean.

Download / Print Talk to our team

Outsourcing BIM works brilliantly when it is set up properly and painfully when it is not. The difference is rarely the modeler; it is the brief, the standards and the expectations agreed up front. Use this checklist to get all three right before you send a single file.

1. Before you start, define the work

  • Agree the scope per discipline (architecture, structure, MEP) and per deliverable.
  • Specify the Level of Detail (LOD) required at each stage, element by element.
  • Share your BIM Execution Plan, templates and CAD/BIM standards (or ask the partner for theirs).
  • Confirm the software, version and file formats for exchange.
  • Fix the coordinate system, units, levels and naming conventions.

2. Evaluate the partner

  • Ask for sample models and drawings in your discipline, not just renders.
  • Check they can work to your standards, not only their own.
  • Confirm their coordination and clash-detection workflow.
  • Review their QA process, who checks the work before it reaches you?
  • Ask about data security, NDAs and how project files are stored.
  • Run a small paid pilot before committing to a large package.

3. Set up the engagement

  • Agree communication cadence, channels and a single point of contact.
  • Define the review and revision process, and how many rounds are included.
  • Set realistic milestones with buffers for coordination.
  • Clarify timezone overlap and turnaround expectations.

4. Protect quality and delivery

  • Require an LOD/scope matrix so nothing is assumed.
  • Insist on coordination reviews, not just a final hand-off.
  • Ask for model + drawings + schedules, all generated from the same model.
  • Verify deliverables against your standards on the first issue, then trust but check.

Red flags to walk away from

Be cautious if a partner cannot show discipline-specific samples, refuses a paid pilot, has no documented QA, is vague about LOD, or promises unrealistically fast turnarounds on coordinated work. Good BIM is a process, and a partner who respects the process is worth far more than one who simply quotes the lowest price.
